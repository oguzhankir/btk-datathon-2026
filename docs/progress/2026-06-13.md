## 1. The evening chain: the models tried and why they didn't hold

| exp | model | result (OOF MSE) | decision |
|---|---|---|---|
| exp018 | mDeBERTa-v3-base | NaN (architectural instability) → switched to XLM-R-base, 132.4 | text floor, 0 weight |
| exp020 | Turkish BERT + **v2 recipe** (attention pool + multi-sample dropout + LLRD + cosine) | 134.9 | recipe didn't break the floor |
| exp021 | XLM-R-base + v2 recipe | 136.9 | recipe didn't break the floor |
| exp019 | CatBoost @ kitchen-sink-v3 | 75.28 | entered the blend (0.352 weight) |

**The mDeBERTa NaN incident (record of decisions):** `AutoModelForSequenceClassification` with mDeBERTa-v3
produced NaN on the first forward pass. The following were tried in sequence → none of them fixed it: (1) gradient clipping +
lowering lr, (2) reinit of the classifier/pooler head with small values + skipping the NaN-loss batch,
(3) `AutoModel` + mean-pool + linear head. The root cause is mDeBERTa's *disentangled attention* +
the v3 *Enhanced Mask Decoder* layer and the numerical instability of the `word_embeddings._weight` weight-tying buffer
in float32. Decision: **abandon** mDeBERTa, switch to the proven-stable XLM-R-base.
In the process, `src/bert_finetune.py` was permanently switched to `AutoModel + mean-pool + linear head` (works
across all backbones, and the classification-head loading warnings disappeared).

**Why the v2 recipe (`src/bert_finetune_v2.py`) was written:** there was a valid objection to the
"6 transformers stalled at 11.5" argument — the 6 models shared **a single weak recipe** (mean-pool + a single
linear head, 5 epochs), i.e. it was really "1 recipe × 3-4 backbones". To refute this,
the recipe was rewritten from scratch: **learned attention pooling**, **multi-sample dropout** (5 masks),
**layer-wise LR decay (LLRD)**, **cosine schedule + warmup**, 8 epochs + inner-val best-epoch
selection. Result: fold RMSEs again 11.1–12.1, OOF MSE 135–137. **It wasn't a recipe problem —
this was the ceiling of the text signal.** This also explains the reason behind §6 below.

## 2. The CV→LB gap: 100% year distribution (NOT overfit/leak)

`floor_analysis.py` §1-2. Separating the OOF residual by year and weighting each year's MSE by the
**test set's year distribution** yields an honest LB estimate:

| year | train_n | test weight | OOF MSE | bias | target variance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2019 | 1289 | 0.040 | 53.9 | +0.27 | 158 |
| 2020 | 1329 | 0.051 | 55.1 | +0.13 | 167 |
| 2021 | 1270 | 0.069 | 61.6 | −0.13 | 201 |
| 2022 | 1293 | 0.088 | 73.6 | +0.07 | 204 |
| 2023 | 1312 | 0.130 | 75.6 | +0.03 | 238 |
| 2024 | 1319 | 0.199 | 74.1 | +0.39 | 237 |
| 2025 | 1191 | 0.220 | 102.9 | −0.16 | 335 |
| 2026 | 997 | 0.203 | 101.0 | −0.55 | 325 |

- **flat OOF MSE = 73.61**, **test-year-weighted = 83.39** ≈ actual **LB 82.96**. ✔
- **Bias ≈ 0 in every year** → the calibration is correct; this is why year-reweighting (exp008)
  hadn't worked — that turned out to be right.
- **Inference:** the +9.3 gap between CV and LB comes entirely from the test being skewed toward late years
  (2024-2026 = 62% of the test) and from the late years being harder. **There is no hidden
  overfit or leak.** The train is distributed ≈evenly across years, the test is late-year weighted; that's the gap.

Figure: `year_error_decomposition.png` (red bar = year MSE, blue line = that year's share within the
test — the error and the test weight peak in the same place).

## 3. The real floor evidence: within-year R² is constant

`floor_analysis.py` §3. The definitive answer to the question "Are we modeling the late years poorly,
or are those years genuinely noisier?" — how much of **each year's own variance** we explain:

| year | target std | OOF MSE | **R²** |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2019 | 12.6 | 53.9 | +0.659 |
| 2021 | 14.2 | 61.6 | +0.693 |
| 2023 | 15.4 | 75.6 | +0.682 |
| 2024 | 15.4 | 74.1 | +0.688 |
| 2025 | 18.3 | 102.9 | +0.693 |
| 2026 | 18.0 | 101.0 | +0.689 |

**R² is ~0.69 in every year — flat.** The error is high in 2026 because the target std is 18.0 (vs 12.6 in 2019),
not because we built a worse model. We extract the same proportion of signal everywhere.
**Inference:** there is no "signal specific to the late years that we're missing." The late-year error is
variance-driven, i.e. irreducible. Figure: `r2_by_year.png`.

## 4. Two intuitive "fixes" — neither wins even as an oracle

`floor_analysis.py` §4. In the late years the ceiling mass rises to 13%; two ideas were tested:

- **Snap the ceiling to 100:** the late-year ceiling RMSE is already 5.41 (the region we predict
  best), only 3.5% of the late-year error. Even an oracle that knows the true 100s gets 102.0→98.5
  (negligible, and moreover we can't know which ones are 100 in the test). → **Rejected** (snap-to-100 had
  been rejected yesterday too, confirmed).
- **Widen the distribution (dispersion rescale):** our predictions are under-dispersed (std 14.5 vs true
  18.2). But scaling to the true std with an oracle gives 102.0 → **110.9 (WORSE).** → Under-dispersion
  is **correct**; regression should shrink toward the mean, and forcing it adds error. **Rejected.**

## 6. Why text is WORSE than tabular — and what that means

`floor_analysis.py` §6. Placing the text side by side with the true score on the highest-error rows:

| what the text says | true score | prediction |
|---|---|---|
| "impressive profile, mastery, strong candidate" (≈95) | **45.2** | 99.6 |
| "high achievement in coding/SQL, but weak project" (≈65) | **95.7** | 56.5 |
| "could become a significant asset" (≈60) | **0.0** | 59.2 |

The text describes the **profile** (skills, projects), **not the score**. Brilliant text
can carry a low score, and vice versa. So `text ≈ f(features)`, `score ≈ g(features) + noise`;
the text is a **noisier** view of the features. This is why our text model (RMSE 11.5) is
worse than tabular (8.7) and why the v2 recipe in §1 couldn't break the floor — **there is no extra signal
in the text beyond the features.** The keyword→mean differences (mükemmel ("excellent")→91.8) are real, but
these are already captured by the TF-IDF ridge meta-feature.

## 7. Is there a forgotten signal? — column coverage + in-text numbers

`floor_analysis.py` §7. Taking the user's "there might be something we're missing" intuition seriously,
two structural checks:

- **All 44 numeric/categorical raw columns** are used in feature engineering; the only column
  not referenced is `mentor_feedback_text` (which is processed separately anyway). → **No skipped column.**
- **Text-table number mismatch:** the text states an internship count in 725 rows, agreeing with the table
  only 17.4% of the time (they contradict) — **but the correlation of this contradiction with the residual is −0.010** (zero).
  → The numbers in the text are not signal either.

The **residual-GBM** test in `reports/eda/residual_analysis.py` (from yesterday) had already said
"there is no nonlinear residual signal in the existing features" (R²≈0). Today's two checks close off the
"there isn't any in the features we never built either" side.

## 8. "People are at 80, we're at 83" — the real composition of the gap

`floor_analysis.py` §8. The public LB is a **subset** of the full test set (typically 20-30%).
The error distribution is heavy-tailed (50+ point deviations on some rows), so the standard error of the
MSE measured on the subset is large:

| public size | SE(MSE) | 95% band |
|---|---|---|
| 20% (n=2000) | ±3.35 | ±6.6 MSE |
| 30% (n=3000) | ±2.74 | ±5.4 MSE |
| 50% (n=5000) | ±2.12 | ±4.2 MSE |

The top-1..top-10 range is **1.22 MSE**, and our gap to #1 is **2.25 MSE** — **both are
within the noise band.** The team currently showing 80.71 could be at 83 on private; we, at 82.96,
could be at 81 on private. Figure: `public_lb_noise.png`.

> **Honest nuance:** all teams are measured on the same public rows, so the *relative* difference
> is less noisy than the absolute MSE. So there may be a **real but small** edge at the top
> (~0.71 R² instead of ~0.69). This is not a "hidden trick we're missing"; it's just
> a margin amounting to sitting a bit closer to the floor itself.

## 9. Decision and final strategy

1. **The modeling front is closed.** 7 independent tests (residual-GBM, within-year R², 6 transformers,
   reading text against score, ceiling/dispersion oracle, column coverage, text-number mismatch) all reach the same
   conclusion: `score = g(features) + irreducible noise`, with RMSE ~8.5 being the floor of that noise.
2. **Don't chase the public LB.** The live ranking is largely subset noise. Overfitting to the public
   (e.g. snap-to-100, pseudo-label, multi-seed text) lowers private — indeed,
   pseudo-label (exp017) had gotten worse on the public too.
3. **The private bet = a robust, CV-supported, correctly-calibrated blend.** Ours is exactly that.
4. **The purpose of today's 5 submissions:** not to search for a model — but to **empirically confirm** the noise and
   pick the most robust candidate for private. All 5 variants are at-floor (CV 73.61–74.02) but built with different
   member-sets/methods (~0.4-0.6 points per row, max 3.1 points difference). If they all move
   within ±0.3 on the LB, the noise is proven and `blend_full_ridge` can be locked in with confidence.

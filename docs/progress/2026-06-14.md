## 0. TL;DR

- **Final public LB (our best): `blend_all` = 82.24** (down from 82.96 the day before — our single
  biggest jump, **−0.72**, from aligning the stacker to the test-year distribution).
- **We finished ~82.2; the entire public top-11 converged to 80.3–81.2** with 27–30 entries each.
- **Honest revision:** a whole field converging to 80–81 is NOT explainable by public-LB noise. It

  Our measured "R²≈0.69 ceiling" is the floor of **our** feature set/approach, **not** a proof of the

  We worked from scratch and never read the competition's `Code`/`Discussion` tabs, so we were outside

## 1. The one thing that actually moved the LB: objective alignment (−0.72)

Day 3's "boring" refinement turned out to be the biggest win of the whole competition. The base
models and blend had been optimizing **uniform** MSE, but the competition scores **test-year-weighted**
MSE (the test set is 62% late years). Aligning the Ridge stacker to that distribution —
`scripts/blend.py --weight-by-test-year --year-conditional` — moved the public LB from 82.96 → 82.24.

| submission | combiner | public LB |
|---|---|---|
| blend_full_ridge | uniform-objective ridge stack | 82.96 |
| **blend_all** | test-year-weighted + year-conditional + all members | **82.24** |
| blend_lbopt | same, without the noisy NN members | 82.28 |
| blend_llm | same + 8B-LLM OOF | 82.29 |

The lesson: once signal is exhausted, the remaining wins come from **aligning the objective to what
the metric actually measures**, not from new models. (This is also why naive per-model year-reweighting,
exp008, failed — it added variance without fixing the *stacker's* objective.)

## 2. Day-4 experiments (all confirmed the OUR-approach floor)

- **8B Turkish LLM, full 5 folds (exp023):** CV MSE 133.8 (RMSE 11.57) — identical to a 110M BERT.
  Added −0.05 to the blend purely as a decorrelated member. Text floor is a *signal* limit, not capacity.
- **Denoising AutoEncoder (exp024):** the canonical Kaggle tabular booster. Most decorrelated member
  (residual corr ~0.78) but too weak standalone (RMSE 10.7) to lift the blend meaningfully.
- **80-trial Optuna on LightGBM (live):** best CV 75.79 → 75.44 → 75.24 → 75.21 → **75.21** (trials
  5/20/40/60/80). The last 20 trials gave **zero** improvement — long HPO plateaus; it is not a lever.
- **NN-leak + feature-twin (`floor_analysis.py` §10):** no near-duplicate test rows; but students with
  near-identical features differ by **~11 points** in target — the same number as every other floor.
- **External data (§11):** impossible — the dataset is synthetic (generated students, generated target,
  LLM-written text); there is no real entity to join against.

Every internal diagnostic says: *given our features*, the model cannot do better than ~R²0.69.

## 3. The honest reckoning — why the field beat us

Here is where day 3 was wrong. We concluded "the gap to the top is mostly public-LB noise." But by
the deadline the **entire top-11 had converged to 80.3–81.2** (28, 19, 28, 27, … entries). A whole
field does not converge to a tight band by luck. That convergence is the signature of a **real,
shared edge**:

1. **A competition-shared trick or leak.** The most common datathon dynamic: someone posts a leak, a
   key feature, or a strong public notebook; scores then drop across the field over days. The temporal
   pattern we watched (steady decline, tight clustering) fits this exactly. We built in isolation and
   never read the `Code`/`Discussion` tabs — so we missed whatever was shared there.
2. **A feature/representation insight we didn't find.** Our residual-GBM/aspect/twin tests rule out
   signal *in the features we constructed*, not signal in a representation we never tried.

What this means for our analysis: the floor we measured is **our** floor. The field demonstrably
reached a lower one. Intellectual honesty requires saying so plainly — we did not crack what the
field cracked, and the most probable reason is that we worked outside the information loop where the
edge was shared.

**Actionable (for the remaining window): read the competition's Kaggle `Code` and `Discussion` tabs.**
That is almost certainly where the field's edge lives.

## 4. What still stands

None of the above diminishes the engineering: a fixed-fold, leakage-audited, fully reproducible
pipeline; a from-scratch comparison of GBMs, an 8B LLM, a DAE, and a Ridge/year-conditional stacker;
and a documented chain of evidence for every decision. The objective-alignment win (−0.72) is a real,
transferable lesson. We were rigorous; we were just not plugged into the shared-trick loop, and we
were overconfident in calling our floor *the* floor.
